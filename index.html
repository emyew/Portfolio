Howl

Project Brief

What is one way we can provide information about on-campus opportunities in one location while creating an environment in which advice and mentorship is encouraged?

Over five weeks, myself and four other students worked on Howl, a mobile web application that we created with undergraduates in mind. Our idea was to create an app that functioned similar to Yelp's review system, with guided questions and a rating system.

Interviewing and Research

Our group compiled questions to ask about their experiences being involved in organizations or building their career success. After reviewing the results, we determined that there was a need for a location that provided a compilation of opportunities as well as a friendly, supportive, and informative environment.

Personas and Storyboards

From the data we gathered, we created personas to illustrate ideally what our app aimed to do. Pictured here is one of my personas Eboni: Motivated incoming freshman who is looking for a method to know what is important in planning out a career path and needs help prioritizing. Another persona created was Ivonne: third year experienced undergraduate who is looking for a credible location to give mentorship while sharing experiences and stories.

Prototyping

In deciding a title inspired by Yelp and our team name, we called our app Howl. Each member of the group had a different interpretation of the logo design. My approach was to go with a angular simplified wolf with a triton on its back to symbolize the connection with UCSD and the emphasis on mentorship. Eventually, we felt that another design worked better. One group member had the idea of creating a keyhole shape to represent the unlocking of opportunities. From that we created our present design.

Our earlier prototypes had different variations as well. It involved a fixed navbar and a side menu. The home page consisted of a visual representation of the categories, which lead to a list of events that could be sorted. The event page itself contained further details as well as the advices.

Final Design

Following our user testing, we had many additions. We included an initial screen that filtered out events according to majors, and included more options in the side menu to make navigation much more smooth. Another addition was the guided feedback form that came with a series of questions to help guide the writer. We also allowed the feedback to be sorted and included an upvote/downvote system on the feedback to ensure that the more helpful/instructive ones reached the top.

NuTriton

Project Brief

How do we help on-campus students with dietary restrictions find suitable locations that have food that meets their limitations?

Over ten weeks our team of three worked on NuTriton, a mobile web application that we created with on-campus undergraduates in mind. We wanted to improve the experience of the UCSD dining hall app feature.

Interviewing and Data Collection

During the week, we conducted online surveys in UCSD affiliated pages as well as in person interviews with random select individuals. What we found was in addition to assisting those with dietary restrictions, the app could prove useful for helping those who also want to have a healthier diet. We also found that being able to locate the nearest food location would make a great added app functionality.

Storyboarding

To help visualize user needs and problems, we created two storyboard scenarios. Both cases involve on-campus likely scenarios.

Paper Prototyping/Heuristic Evaluation

To simulate the user experience, we created a paper prototype to help us quickly iterate through different designs and try new features. Our designs were quickly put to the test in heuristic evaluations, where we quickly found and solved design issues. One such example was the tab navigation.

User Testing

As our app continue to progress in development, we began user testing to find more design issues. While testing, we observed the user's behavior to determine if there were features that needed to be simplified or changed. One such change was the size and positioning of several elements that lead to the map feature being too long on the page to allow easy scrolling.

A/B Testing

Nearing finalization, we used Chi Squared Testing to compare two different versions of our Food Finder page. We tested plain links against boxed links as well as single column links and double column links.
